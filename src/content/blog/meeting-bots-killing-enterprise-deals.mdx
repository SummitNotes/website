A sales team shared this on Reddit recently:

> We used Fireflies and Granola for our SMB calls — no problem. Then we got our first enterprise prospect. The VP saw the bot in the call, asked who invited it, and when we explained it was our note-taking tool, he said "we don't allow third-party recording on our calls" and ended the meeting.

Deal dead. Because of a bot.

This isn't a one-off story. It's becoming a pattern — and enterprise sales teams are starting to take notice.

## Why Enterprise Buyers React Differently

SMB buyers generally don't care about meeting bots. They're used to them. Half their own team probably uses Otter or Fireflies anyway.

Enterprise is different. Enterprise buyers have:

- **Legal teams** that review what third parties can record
- **Security policies** that restrict what software can access their meetings
- **Procurement processes** that require vendor DPAs before any data sharing
- **Industries** — finance, healthcare, legal — where recording is heavily regulated

When your meeting bot shows up unannounced to a VP call, you're not just being slightly rude. You're potentially violating their acceptable use policy before the deal has even started.

## The Trust Problem Before the Trust Problem

In sales, trust is everything. You're asking a prospect to trust you with their business processes, their data, their budget.

The bot undermines that from the first minute.

Before you've said a word about your product, you've demonstrated that you'll bring third-party tools into their environment without asking. You've shown you'll outsource their conversation to a cloud service they didn't vet. You've made them wonder what else you'll do without permission.

That VP didn't walk out because he's anti-technology. He walked out because the bot was a proxy for a bigger concern: *can I trust this vendor?*

## What's Actually Happening on Their End

When Fireflies or Granola joins your call, here's what happens on the enterprise side:

1. An unknown account joins their Zoom/Teams/Meet session
2. That account records audio and video
3. The recording is uploaded to a third-party server
4. AI processes the content — potentially to train their models
5. A transcript is generated and stored in someone's cloud account

Many enterprise security policies explicitly prohibit steps 2-5 without prior approval. Their IT team may have automated alerts for this.

You triggered a security incident before your demo started.

## The Fix Is Simple

Don't use a bot.

Record your own audio locally. Transcribe it on your device. Keep the notes in your own files.

This means:
- Nothing joins the call
- Nothing uploads to third-party servers
- Nothing appears in their security logs
- Nothing requires their legal team to review a vendor DPA

You take great notes. They never even know you're taking notes. The conversation stays between the people in it.
