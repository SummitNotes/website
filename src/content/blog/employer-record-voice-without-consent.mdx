The short answer is: it depends on where you are, who's recording, and what they do with the data. The longer answer involves federal wiretapping law, a patchwork of state consent rules, and a relatively new wrinkle — AI meeting bots that do things traditional recording never did.

Let's go through it.

## Federal Baseline: The Wiretap Act

The Electronic Communications Privacy Act (18 U.S.C. § 2511) — colloquially known as the federal wiretapping law — makes it illegal to intentionally intercept wire, oral, or electronic communications.

The key exception: **one-party consent**. Federal law allows recording if at least one party to the conversation consents. That party can be the person doing the recording. So an employer can legally record a conversation under federal law if they're a party to it.

This creates the baseline: in a state with no additional restrictions, an employer who is part of a meeting can record it under federal law without telling anyone else.

But "federal law allows it" is a low bar, and most employers don't operate in a vacuum.

## One-Party vs. All-Party Consent States

States layer on top of federal law. The key distinction:

**One-party consent states** (federal standard applies): The recorder counts as a consenting party. Recording is legal without notifying others.

**All-party (two-party) consent states**: Everyone in the conversation must consent. Recording secretly is a crime.

All-party consent states include:
- **California** (Penal Code § 632)
- **Florida** (§ 934.03)
- **Illinois** (720 ILCS 5/14-2)
- **Maryland** (Cts. & Jud. Proc. § 10-402)
- **Massachusetts** (G.L. c. 272 § 99)
- **Michigan** (MCL 750.539c)
- **Montana** (MCA 45-8-213)
- **Nevada** (NRS 200.650)
- **New Hampshire** (RSA 570-A:2)
- **Oregon** (ORS 165.540)
- **Pennsylvania** (18 Pa. C.S. § 5703)
- **Washington** (RCW 9.73.030)

In these states, recording employees in meetings without their knowledge is potentially a criminal act — not just a civil liability. In California, violations carry fines up to $2,500 and jail time up to a year.

The practical implication: an employer in California who deploys an AI meeting tool that records without explicit notice to all participants could be violating California Penal Code § 632 before biometric laws even enter the picture.

## Workplace Monitoring Exceptions — And Their Limits

Courts have recognized that workplaces are different. Employees generally have reduced privacy expectations at work compared to their personal lives. Common exceptions to standard wiretapping rules:

**Consent via employment agreement:** Many employers include recording consent in employment contracts or policies. Courts have sometimes upheld this as sufficient for federal law purposes. For all-party consent states, it's murkier — a general employment policy may not constitute specific informed consent for recording.

**Equipment monitoring exception:** The "business extension" exception under federal law allows employers to monitor calls on company equipment. This applies more cleanly to telephone monitoring than to modern AI meeting tools.

**What doesn't apply:** These exceptions cover the employer's own employees. They don't cover external participants — clients, vendors, contractors, or anyone who didn't sign your employment agreement. Those people are a separate consent problem.

## Where AI Meeting Bots Change Everything

Traditional recording — hitting "record" in Zoom, using a dedicated recorder — is relatively well-understood under wiretapping law. AI meeting bots introduce new wrinkles.

**The bot-joins problem:** When an AI notetaker bot joins your meeting as a participant, it may not be obvious to all attendees what's happening. Several lawsuits have been filed arguing that bot attendance without clear disclosure violates wiretapping and consent laws, regardless of employer authority to record.

**Speaker identification and biometrics:** The bigger issue is what the recording is used for. If the AI tool creates voiceprints for speaker attribution, you're no longer just recording a conversation — you're collecting biometric identifiers. Under BIPA (Illinois), CUBI (Texas), and similar laws, this requires written consent before collection, period. Workplace monitoring exceptions don't override BIPA.

**Post-meeting processing:** A 2026 Fortune investigation documented AI meeting assistants remaining on calls after participants dropped, recording off-the-record conversations. Whatever your legal basis for recording the meeting, staying on after it ends to record what people say when they think no one is listening is a different legal matter entirely.

**Training data:** Some AI meeting tools use recorded content to improve their models. If employees' voices are being used to train AI systems without their knowledge — especially when those voices create biometric identifiers — that's potentially a BIPA and GDPR issue on top of wiretapping law.

## What Employees Can Actually Do

If you suspect your employer is recording you in ways that violate your rights:

**Check state law first.** If you're in a two-party consent state and recordings are happening without your knowledge, that's potentially criminal — not just a policy violation. Document what you can.

**Review your employment agreement.** Did you sign a consent to monitoring? What does it actually cover? "Recording for training purposes" is not the same as "creating biometric voiceprints used to train AI models."

**Ask directly.** Employees in organizations subject to GDPR have the right to ask what personal data is being collected and how it's being used. In CCPA-covered California, similar rights apply. A written response to a written request creates a paper trail.

**Refuse to be recorded.** In all-party consent states, you can withhold consent. Retaliation for exercising your privacy rights is a separate legal issue.

**Consult an employment attorney.** Particularly if you're in Illinois and believe voiceprints are being collected without written consent — BIPA allows individual lawsuits with significant statutory damages.
